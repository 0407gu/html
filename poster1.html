Wide-Field, Artifact-Suppressed Lensless Microscopy

Via Feature-Agnostic Autofocus and Multi-Target Separable Phase Retrieval

Ziyang Li, Xuyang Zhou, Sida Gao, Guancheng Huang, et al., and Zhengjun Liu*
School of Physics, Harbin Institute of Technology (HIT), China
HIT
1. Abstract & Motivation

Lensless on-chip microscopy (LOCM) is ideal for high-throughput imaging but often struggles with sample-sensor distance variations and cumulative noise.

A robust framework is proposed to address these challenges by integrating:

  • Feature-Agnostic Autofocus: Handles non-planar samples effectively.
  • Multi-Target Separable (MTS) Phase Retrieval: Separates signal from background artifacts.
  • Natural Support Constraints: Utilizes overlapping stacks to ensure continuity.
"The proposed method eliminates reliance on stringent calibration and provides a robust solution for high-throughput computational imaging."
2. System Configuration

A portable, lensless setup is designed for non-darkroom scenarios.

  • Source: Fiber laser (λ = 532 nm).
  • Detector: CMOS sensor mounted on a 1D manual translation stage.
  • Data Acquisition: 5 intensity frames captured at varying axial distances.
  • Robustness: The algorithm compensates for platform vibration, stray light, and diverging motion trajectories.

3. Complex Field Correction

A "Divide-and-Conquer" strategy with Complex Field Correction is employed to rectify geometric distortions.

A. Feature-Agnostic Autofocus
Conventional metrics fail on sparse samples. TV-regularized Single-Shot Phase Retrieval (SSPR) is utilized. This method accurately determines the axial distance even for small (300×300 pixel) sub-regions with limited features.

B. Segment-Dependent Registration
This step corrects for "diffraction scale magnification" (wavefront expansion).

  • Coarse Registration: Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT).
  • Fine Registration: Fourier Cross-Correlation (FCC) for sub-pixel accuracy.

4. MTS Phase Retrieval

To eliminate artifacts, the Multi-Target Separable (MTS) algorithm is introduced. The physical imaging model is defined as:

u = p ⊙ (x + n)

Where u is the measured field, p is the probe (illumination), x is the sample, and n is the background noise.

  1. Model: Multi-objective optimization treating x, p, and n as separate variables.
  2. Optimization: Momentum-accelerated stochastic gradient descent (Nesterov's acceleration).
  3. Advantage: Uses "Error Prediction" to mitigate noise accumulation, offering superior performance over Multi-Frame Alternating Projection (MFAP).
5. Pixel Super-Resolution

The proposed method achieves significant resolution enhancement beyond the Nyquist-Shannon sampling limit.

  • Resolution: Achieves 775 nm half-pitch resolution (resolving Group 9, Element 3).
  • Enhancement: A 2.15-fold improvement in spatial resolution.
  • Artifact Removal: Effectively eliminates "striped artifacts" common in conventional reconstruction.

6. Biological Validation

The system was validated on large-scale biological samples with complex topography.

  • Full FOV: 28.6 mm2 (Macro-scale imaging).
  • Imaging Depth: > 80 μm (An order of magnitude larger than typical 5-10 μm slices).
  • Samples: Unstained Chronic Gastric Ulcer & H&E Stained Thyroid Adenocarcinoma.
  • Compatibility: Successfully applied to Coherent Diffraction Imaging (CDI) with a 125 μm depth.
7. Conclusion

A versatile framework is presented that transforms a simple lensless setup into a high-fidelity imager. It enables label-free, pixel-super-resolved imaging that is robust to environmental disturbances, making it highly suitable for digital pathology and clinical diagnostics.
